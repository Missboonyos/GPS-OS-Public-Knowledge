# Principles

Core beliefs provide direction. Principles guide action.

The following principles define how GPS OS approaches problem solving, product design, and software development. They are intended to help teams make consistent decisions across different domains and technologies.

## 1. Start with the Problem

Every project begins with understanding the problem — not choosing the technology.

Before discussing features, AI, databases, or programming languages, first ask:

- What problem are we trying to solve?
- Who experiences this problem?
- Why does it happen?
- How is it handled today?

A clear understanding of the problem is the foundation of every successful product.

## 2. Understand the Workflow Before Designing Software

Software should reflect how work actually happens.

Observe the complete workflow before proposing a solution.

Identify:

- People involved
- Responsibilities
- Information flow
- Decisions
- Pain points
- Risks
- Dependencies

Improving a workflow often creates more value than adding new features.

## 3. Design for People

Technology serves people — not the other way around.

Every design decision should reduce confusion, unnecessary effort, and opportunities for error.

Good software should make work feel more natural, not more complicated.

## 4. Turn Knowledge into Systems

Operational knowledge is valuable only when it can be shared and reused.

Document important decisions. Capture business rules. Transform experience into repeatable processes.

Systems should preserve knowledge rather than depend on individual memory.

## 5. Let Evidence Guide Decisions

Avoid assumptions whenever possible.

Understand the reasoning behind a proposed solution.

Validate ideas with observation, real workflows, user feedback, or trusted sources before scaling them.

Confidence should come from evidence, not intuition alone.

## 6. Prefer Simplicity Over Complexity

Complex solutions often introduce unnecessary risks.

Choose the simplest solution that solves the real problem.

Simplicity improves maintainability, usability, and reliability.

## 7. Build Iteratively

Do not attempt to build the perfect product on the first release.

Start with the smallest solution that delivers meaningful value.

Observe. Learn. Improve. Repeat.

## 8. Design Security from the Beginning

Security is part of the architecture — not a feature added later.

Protect sensitive information. Collect only the data that is necessary.

Design systems with privacy, reliability, and trust in mind from the beginning.

## 9. Document to Preserve Thinking

Documentation is more than project management.

Its purpose is to preserve decisions, assumptions, lessons learned, and knowledge that would otherwise be lost.

Well-written documentation enables continuous improvement.

## 10. Improve Continuously

No workflow is perfect. No system is final.

Every project is an opportunity to observe, learn, refine, and improve.

Continuous improvement is not an activity after launch.

It is part of the design itself.

## Closing

GPS OS principles are intentionally technology-independent.

Whether building software, improving workflows, or designing business systems, these principles remain the same.

Technology may evolve. Good thinking should endure.
